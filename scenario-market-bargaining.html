Market Bargaining

You do not need to become a grandmaster of souk theatre. You just need enough Darija to ask the price, react without freezing, push back lightly, and leave with dignity still attached.

This page gives you the useful starter language for market bargaining in a way that feels practical rather than dramatic.

What you’ll hear back

In markets, the hard part is often not your first phrase — it’s hearing the seller’s confidence and not immediately collapsing into agreement.

Panic-proof bargaining sequence

If your bargaining courage is thin, this is enough to get through a basic price exchange without drama.

Use this if you blank:

1. kam hada?
2. ghali bzzaf
3. neqqs shwiya, 3afak
4. safi, khllih or bghit hada

That already gives you a functioning market spine.

Mini dialogue

You
كام هادا؟
kam hada?
How much is this?
Seller
غالي؟ لا، ماشي غالي
ghali? la, mashi ghali
Expensive? No, it’s not expensive.
You
غالي بزاف. نقّص شوية، عفاك
ghali bzzaf. neqqs shwiya, 3afak
It’s too expensive. Lower it a little, please.
You
صافي، بغيت هادا
safi, bghit hada
Okay, I want this one.

How a real person would say it

textbook
كم ثمن هذا؟
kam thaman hadha?
Understandable in a formal Arabic universe. Not the vibe you want in a normal market interaction.
natural
كام هادا؟
kam hada?
Short, clear, and completely usable.
real reaction
غالي بزاف
ghali bzzaf
This is where the interaction gets more human: not just asking, but reacting.

The best default target here is the natural version: normal, useful, and unlikely to make you sound stiff or accidentally theatrical. The textbook version helps you understand why some resources sound off. The slang / clipped version is for later, once your ear is stronger.

Quick practice

Mini challenge: You see something you want, but the first price feels too high. A very serviceable sequence is:

kam hada? ... ghali bzzaf ... neqqs shwiya, 3afak ... safi, bghit hada.

That is enough to sound engaged, not helpless, and not weirdly overdramatic.
