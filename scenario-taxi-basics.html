Taxi Basics

One of the fastest ways to feel less lost in Morocco is to handle a taxi ride without defaulting to English, panic, or interpretive hand gestures. This page focuses on the useful Darija that gets you moving, clarifies destination, and keeps the interaction normal.

We’re starting with the safest, most useful lines first. Later this can expand into fuller roleplay, listening drills, and more natural/slangier variants.

What you’ll hear back

Half the challenge is not what you say. It’s understanding the short replies that come back fast and casually.

Panic-proof sequence

If your brain suddenly becomes decorative, this mini sequence is enough to get through a basic taxi ride without needing elegance.

Use this if you blank:

1. salam
2. bghit nmshi l-...
3. wqef hna, 3afak
4. shukran

That is already enough to be functional. Beautiful? Maybe not. Useful? Absolutely.

Mini dialogue

This is the shape of a more complete, very normal taxi interaction.

Driver
سلام، لفين؟
salam, l-fin?
Hi, where to?
You
بغيت نمشي للمعاريف
bghit nmshi l-Maarif
I want to go to Maarif.
Driver
واش قريب ولا بعيد؟
wash qrib wlla b3id?
Is it near or far?
You
قريب من هنا. وقف هنا، عفاك.
qrib mn hna. wqef hna, 3afak.
It’s near here. Stop here, please.
Driver
هنا؟
hna?
Here?
You
آه، وقف هنا، عفاك
ah, wqef hna, 3afak
Yes, stop here please.
You
شكرا
shukran
Thank you.

Natural vs textbook vs slang-ish

One of the things BoerDarija should eventually do really well is show not just what something means, but how normal it sounds.

textbook
أريد الذهاب إلى المعاريف
urid adhhab ila l-Maarif
Understandable, but much too formal and classroom-ish for a normal taxi interaction.
natural
بغيت نمشي للمعاريف
bghit nmshi l-Maarif
This is the safe, useful version. Normal, practical, and exactly the kind of thing you want first.
clipped / contextual
للمعاريف، عفاك
l-Maarif, 3afak
Shorter and more clipped. Useful later, once you’re comfortable enough not to sound like you’re assembling language out of spare parts.

The best default target here is the natural version: normal, useful, and unlikely to make you sound stiff or accidentally theatrical. The textbook version helps you understand why some resources sound off. The slang / clipped version is for later, once your ear is stronger.

Quick practice

Mini challenge: imagine the driver asks "l-fin?" What would you say if you wanted to go to Maarif and then stop near a café?

A good simple answer might be:
bghit nmshi l-Maarif ... wqef hna, 3afak.

Not elegant poetry. Just useful survival language. Which, frankly, is a more respectable goal than poetry in a taxi.
